Caution: Normal Apgar Scores and Prompt Legal Action for Birth Injuries

Can reassuring Apgar scores alone predict neurologic outcomes in newborns?

Robert Painter
May 7, 2024

The birth of a baby boy or girl is a milestone event of life that every parent remembers. In the hustle and bustle of labor and delivery, a lot of things happen. One of the first assessments performed on a newborn is the Apgar score, a quick evaluation that’s been around since the 1950s to look at the baby’s overall health.

However, it’s crucial to understand that a normal Apgar score doesn’t necessarily mean that the baby has not suffered a brain injury during labor and delivery. If your child exhibits developmental issues or delays, it’s essential to seek legal counsel promptly and not be lulled into a false sense of security by normal Apgar scores.

Can Reassuring Apgar Scores Alone Predict Neurologic Outcomes in Newborns?

In its publications, the American College of Obstetricians and Gynecologists (ACOG) has acknowledged the limitations of relying solely on Apgar scores to predict neurologic outcomes in newborns. In their guidelines, ACOG states that while Apgar scores provide a convenient shorthand for reporting a newborn’s status, they’re limited in their ability to predict individual newborn neurologic outcomes. In other words, unfortunately, normal Apgar scores don’t mean that a newborn didn’t suffer a labor and delivery injury, such as hypoxic-ischemic encephalopathy.

In fact, ACOG emphasizes that Apgar scores alone are insufficient to establish or rule out hypoxic injury or potential neurologic impairment. The organization recommends that healthcare providers consider the entire clinical scenario, including factors during labor, delivery, and neonatal course, rather than depending on Apgar scores as the sole predictive measure.

ACOG’s stance underscores the need for comprehensive evaluation and monitoring, even with reassuring Apgar scores, to identify potential neurologic issues in newborns. For parents, this means keeping a careful eye on their new baby to make sure things are on track, and being diligent to investigate matters when something seems off.

The Limitations of Apgar Scores and the Reality of Birth Injuries

While the Apgar score is a valuable tool for assessing a newborn’s immediate condition, it has limitations and doesn’t provide a comprehensive evaluation of the baby’s overall health or definitively rule out the possibility of birth injuries, particularly those affecting the brain.

Brain injuries during labor and delivery can occur because of various factors, including oxygen deprivation (hypoxia or anoxia), placenta problems, uterus problems, trauma, or other complications. Read how the role of cord blood gases in birth injury and medical malpractice cases.

These injuries aren’t always immediately apparent or reflected in the Apgar scores, as newborns can be remarkably resilient, and their bodies may initially compensate for any deficits. And when a diagnosis isn’t made quickly after delivery, it’s easy to get overlooked for one, two, three, or more years as the baby grows and develops. The effects of these injuries can manifest later in life, potentially leading to developmental delays, cognitive or physical impairments, and long-term consequences for the child’s quality of life.

The Importance of Prompt Legal Action and Preserving Parental Claims

In Texas, parents have a limited window of opportunity to pursue legal action on behalf of their child for birth injuries. Under Texas law, parents own the claims for their child for all damages up until the age of 18. If those claims aren’t filed within the two-year statute of limitations, they become time-barred.

Failing to take prompt legal action can have severe consequences —much of the necessary care, therapy, and interventions for brain-injured babies occurs within the first 18 years of life. Early intervention programs, therapies, and specialized medical care are crucial for supporting the child’s development and optimizing outcomes.

By consulting with a Texas birth injury attorney as soon as developmental issues or concerns arise, parents can protect their legal rights and ensure that their child receives the necessary support and compensation for the long-term care and expenses associated with birth injuries.

The Role of Birth Injury Attorneys

Birth injury attorneys play a unique and vital role in investigating and advocating for the rights of children and families affected by labor and delivery and birth injuries. They have the expertise and resources to thoroughly investigate the circumstances surrounding the labor and delivery, identify any potential negligence or malpractice, and pursue legal action on behalf of the child and parents.

Birth injury attorneys understand the complexities of these cases and the long-term implications of birth injuries on a child’s development and quality of life. They can help families navigate the legal system, gather and present evidence, and seek compensation for medical expenses, ongoing care, lost wages, and other damages.

Seeking Justice and Ensuring Adequate Care

Pursuing legal action in birth injury medical malpractice cases isn’t only about seeking justice and holding negligent parties accountable but also about ensuring that the child receives the necessary care and support throughout his or her life.

Brain injuries sustained during labor and delivery can have lifelong consequences, requiring ongoing medical treatment, rehabilitation, assistive devices, specialized education, and often even 24/7 care. The compensation obtained through a successful birth injury lawsuit can help alleviate the financial burden on families and provide the resources needed to ensure the child’s needs are met.

Further, by holding healthcare providers and institutions accountable, birth injury lawsuits help improve patient care and safety by driving improvements in hospital and medical practices, protocols, and patient safety measures, ultimately reducing the risk of future birth injuries and promoting better outcomes for mothers and babies.
